Of the six features Florida's wind mitigation form credits, roof shape is the strange one. Every other feature is something you can add to a house: clips, nails, underlayment, shutters. Roof shape is the house. It was decided the day the plans were drawn, it earns one of the form's meaningful credits, and for most homeowners it is effectively permanent. This guide explains what the hip roof credit is, how inspectors classify a roof, and what is actually actionable about a feature you mostly cannot change.

Hip versus gable, in plain terms

A hip roof slopes upward from all four sides of the house; the roof planes lean against each other like a pyramid, with no vertical roof-end walls. A gable roof slopes on two sides and ends in flat, triangular wall sections (the gables) on the other two. Walk down any Florida street and you can sort most houses into one or the other in a glance: the pyramid-ish roofs are hips, the ones with triangle end-walls are gables.

The reason the form cares is aerodynamic and structural. A hip roof presents a sloped surface to wind from every direction, while a gable end presents a flat vertical face, and the gable-end framing has to resist that load. On the wind mitigation form, the hip shape earns the strongest roof shape credit. The form's credit tables, updated in the April 2026 revision based on the 2024 Residential Wind-Loss Mitigation Study commissioned by the Office of Insurance Regulation, continue to treat shape as one of the credited categories alongside attachment hardware, deck nailing, water resistance, code era, and opening protection.

How the inspector classifies your roof

Real houses are rarely textbook diagrams. Florida homes mix hip sections with gable sections, flat porch roofs, and other geometry, so the form's classification turns on measurement rather than vibes: the inspector evaluates the roof's perimeter and how much of it is non-hip geometry, then assigns the shape category the measurements support. This is one of many places where the 2026 form's documentation posture matters. The inspector photographs the roof and records the geometry, and under the revised form, features that cannot be supported through clear visual inspection or records generally do not receive credit. A homeowner cannot argue a roof into a better category; the geometry is the geometry.

Two practical notes from how the classification works. First, additions change the answer. A gabled room addition or a porch structure tied into the main roof changes the perimeter math, and a home that classified as hip before an addition may not afterward. Second, if your home's roof is a hip and your old wind mitigation report says otherwise, or says nothing, a new inspection is the only way the credit reaches your premium, because the carrier credits the form, not the house.

What the credit is worth

No honest source will quote you a dollar figure for the shape credit alone, because credits are applied by your carrier, from its filed tables, to the windstorm portion of your premium. The windstorm portion typically represents 30 to 60 percent of a Florida premium, and the full stack of documented mitigation features commonly reduces that portion by 25 to 45 percent. Roof shape is one contributor inside those ranges, the discount tables vary by carrier, and the 2026 revision rebalanced credit values to current building science, with some features earning more and some less than before. The requirement that carriers credit documented features at all comes from s. 627.0629, F.S., and since October 2023 every residential insurer must describe its available mitigation discounts on its website, so you can look up how your own carrier treats roof shape.

Can you do anything about a gable roof?

Mostly, no, and this guide will not pretend otherwise. Reshaping a roof from gable to hip is major construction, and nobody undertakes it to chase an insurance credit. The honest framing is different: roof shape is the credit you check, not the credit you buy.

Three things are genuinely actionable. First, verify the classification is right, especially if your report predates an addition, a reroof, or the 2026 form. Second, if your home is gabled, concentrate on the features that are purchasable: roof-to-wall attachment, roof deck attachment, secondary water resistance, and opening protection all have their own credits and their own guides on this site, and none of them depends on shape. Third, if a reroof is in your future anyway, that project is the moment most other roof credits get created or documented, and the new roof's permit trail will serve every later inspection. The 2026 form guide covers why that paper trail now decides credits.

For low- and moderate-income homeowners, the My Safe Florida Home program matches money spent on exactly those purchasable categories (roof-to-wall connections, deck attachment, secondary water resistance, and opening protection) at 2:1 up to $10,000, after a free program inspection identifies what the house needs. Roof shape is not on the eligible improvements list, for the obvious reason: the program funds hardening, not architecture. Details are at mysafeflhome.com.

Check what your current report says

Before any of the above, pull out your existing wind mitigation report, if you have one, and read the roof shape line. Three findings are worth acting on. If the report classifies your roof as hip and your premium has never reflected a shape credit, call your carrier and ask how the report was applied; since October 2023 every residential insurer has been required to describe its mitigation discounts on its website, so you can see the credit structure yourself before the call. If the report predates an addition or other change to the roofline, it describes a house that no longer exists, and the classification may have moved in either direction. And if you have no report at all, the shape credit is among the reasons the inspection tends to pay for itself: it costs around $100 to $150, takes under an hour for most homes, and documents every credited category at once, not just shape. A report stays valid up to five years if nothing material changes, so a single accurate snapshot carries several renewals.

If you are house shopping, this credit is free information

The one moment a buyer truly chooses a roof shape is at purchase. Two otherwise similar Florida houses, one hip and one gable, will carry different windstorm ratings for the life of ownership, and the hip house's advantage costs nothing to maintain. A wind mitigation report on a home you are buying, or the seller's existing report if it is current, tells you the shape classification along with every other credited feature before you commit. A report is valid up to five years if nothing material changed, so a seller's recent report is usable evidence.

The summary

Hip roofs earn the strongest shape credit on the wind mitigation form because of how they meet wind. The classification is measured, photographed, and documented by a licensed inspector, and under the 2026 form the documentation is what your carrier credits. You probably cannot change your roof's shape, but you can make sure it is classified correctly, stack the credits that are purchasable, and let the shape credit be what it is: a fixed fact about your house that should be accurately reflected in your premium.
