Wind mitigation reports age out. A report is valid for up to five years, provided no material changes are made to the structure and no inaccuracies are found, and when yours lapses the credits it carried stop protecting your premium. The April 2026 revision of the inspection form added a second clock to think about: even an unexpired report now describes your home in the vocabulary of a form the state has replaced. This guide sorts out the cases. When an old report still does its job, when a new inspection is clearly worth the money, and how to decide in the ambiguous middle.

The five-year rule, precisely

The validity rule has three parts, and all three matter. A report is valid up to five years. The structure must not have materially changed since the inspection. And the report must not contain inaccuracies. Hit any of the three and the report's authority weakens: past five years it lapses outright; after a material change it no longer describes your house; and a report found inaccurate can be set aside regardless of age.

Notice what the rule implies in both directions. An old report that is accurate and current with your house keeps working for its full five years, including reports completed before the April 2026 form revision. You are not required to re-inspect because the form changed. But the rule also means a report can die early: the homeowner who replaced a roof in year two of a five-year report is carrying paper that no longer matches the house.

Case one: your report simply expired

If five years have run, the decision is mostly made for you. Without a valid report, your carrier has no current documentation of your home's mitigation features, and the credits that depend on documentation are at risk at renewal. A new inspection typically costs around $100 to $150 and takes under an hour for most homes, set against credits that apply to the windstorm portion of your premium, which in Florida typically runs 30 to 60 percent of the bill, with documented features commonly reducing that portion by 25 to 45 percent. For a home that earned credits before, re-establishing the documentation is among the most predictable returns in homeownership. The savings estimator does this arithmetic for your own premium.

One new wrinkle: your re-inspection happens on the revised form, which requires more documentation than the visit you remember. Photos, product approval numbers, and permit records now support every credited feature. Gather your permits and product paperwork before the inspector arrives; the guide to what happens during an inspection covers the preparation.

Case two: your report is valid but predates improvements

This is the expensive case to ignore. Reports are snapshots, and improvements made after the snapshot earn nothing. If you have replaced the roof, added impact windows or doors, installed shutters, upgraded the garage door, or had connection or deck work done since your last inspection, those features are invisible to your carrier until a new report documents them. The five-year rule does not require you to wait out the old report; you can commission a new inspection at any time, and after meaningful improvements you almost certainly should. A reroof alone can touch several credited categories at once (deck attachment, secondary water resistance, and the code era of the roof), which is a lot of undocumented credit to leave sitting.

The 2026 form sharpens this case further. Improvements made since your last inspection may warrant a new inspection under the updated form, and recent permitted work is exactly the kind of evidence the new form is built to credit: the permits are fresh, the product approvals are in hand, and the work is visible. The homeowner with a two-year-old reroof and a six-year-old report is the clearest re-inspection candidate there is.

Case three: your report is valid, the house is unchanged, and the form moved on

Here is the genuinely ambiguous middle, and the honest answer is: it depends on what a re-inspection would find. Your pre-April-2026 report can remain valid for its full five years, and carriers are expected to begin applying credits based on the new form around July 2026 for new reports. The revision rebalanced the discount tables to current building science, meaning some features may earn more under the new form and others less, and it tightened documentation standards, meaning a feature your old report credited loosely might be harder to credit now if your paperwork is thin.

So a re-inspection under the new form is not automatically a win. Two questions do most of the deciding. First, can you document your features? If your permits and product approvals are in order, the new form holds no terror, and features the old form undervalued may do better. If your credited features were documented loosely and the records are gone, your existing valid report may be worth keeping for its remaining life. Second, how much report life is left? With four years remaining, waiting costs little and lets the new form's carrier filings settle in. With one year left, you are re-inspecting soon regardless, and the timing question collapses.

Timing a re-inspection around your renewal

A new report does nothing until your carrier applies it, so sequence the steps with your policy calendar in mind. Working backward from your renewal date: gather your permits and product documentation first, since under the 2026 form they decide what gets credited; book the inspection with enough lead time for the completed form to reach your carrier and be processed before the renewal is priced; and if a qualifying project is mid-flight, let it finish and close out its permits before inspecting, because a report taken mid-project documents the unfinished house. If your current report is still valid, there is no gap to fear: it keeps carrying your credits until the new one supersedes it. The expensive pattern is the opposite one, an expired report and a renewal arriving in the same month, which converts a $100 to $150 errand into a missed pricing cycle. Carriers have been required since October 2023 to describe their mitigation discounts on their websites, so you can also see what the new form's credits look like at your own carrier before deciding when to book.

A note for buyers and sellers

Reports attach to the house's features, and a recent accurate report is usable evidence in a transaction. A seller holding a current report with documented credits is holding a small, legitimate selling point: the buyer's insurer gets documentation from day one. A buyer offered an older report should run the three-part validity test against it, ask the seller for the permit and product paperwork behind its credits (which the 2026 form makes valuable), and treat a report that predates known work on the house as describing a house that no longer exists.

The decision, compressed

Expired report: re-inspect, with your documents gathered. Valid report, improved house: re-inspect; the credits from the improvements are waiting on the paperwork. Valid report, unchanged house: check your documentation and your remaining report life before paying for a new snapshot, and remember the old one keeps working in the meantime. In every case, the inspection itself is the cheap part. What the 2026 system rewards is the homeowner whose paper trail matches their house, and the regulator's own materials at the OIR wind mitigation resources page are the place to confirm the current rules.
