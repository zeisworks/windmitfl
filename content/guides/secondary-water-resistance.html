Most of the wind mitigation form is about keeping a roof on a house. Secondary water resistance is about what happens if the storm starts winning anyway. It is the only credited feature whose entire job is damage limitation: a sealed barrier under the roof covering so that when shingles or tiles are stripped, the house below does not take on water through the deck. On the form it is usually abbreviated SWR, in the trade it is usually called peel-and-stick, and it earns its own credit in the form's feature categories. This guide explains what it is, why it almost always gets installed during a reroof, and how the credit and the grant money work.

What secondary water resistance is

A roof sheds water with its covering: shingles, tiles, or metal. Under that covering sits the deck, the structural sheathing panels, and the seams between those panels are the problem. If wind strips the covering, rain falls on bare decking and pours through every seam and nail penetration into the attic and the living space below. Much of the damage in a windstorm loss is exactly this: not the roof structure failing, but water entering after the covering is gone.

Secondary water resistance is the backup layer that addresses it. The credited version is a peel-and-stick underlayment: a self-adhering membrane applied to the roof deck, sealing the panel seams so the deck itself sheds water even with the covering stripped. The membrane is the "secondary" in the name; the covering is the primary water barrier, and the SWR is what keeps a covering failure from becoming an interior loss.

Why it is a reroof decision

The membrane is applied directly to the deck, beneath everything else on the roof. That means there is exactly one convenient moment in a roof's life to install it: during a reroof, after the old covering is stripped and before the new one goes on. As a standalone project it would mean removing a functional roof covering to add a layer beneath it, which is why, in practice, almost no one does it that way.

This makes SWR the canonical example of a rule that runs through all of this site's feature guides: reroofs are when credits get created. The deck is exposed, so the nailing can be brought to current standards (the deck attachment credit), the membrane can go down (this credit), and the roof-to-wall connections are at their most accessible (the attachment credit). A homeowner planning a reroof who has not asked the contractor about peel-and-stick is leaving a named, credited feature on the table at the one moment it is cheap to add.

How the credit is documented, especially after 2026

SWR has always had a verification problem: once the new covering is installed, the membrane is hidden. An inspector may find evidence at the edges or from the attic side, but the cleanest proof is documentary, and under the form revised effective April 1, 2026 that distinction now decides credits. The 2026 form requires credited features to be supported by photos, product approval numbers, and permit records, and features that cannot be documented through a permit or clear visual inspection generally will not receive credit.

For SWR, that translates into homeowner homework during the reroof, not after. Photograph the membrane on the deck before the covering goes on, or ask the contractor to. Keep the product name and approval documentation. Keep the contract line item and the permit records. A peel-and-stick layer that exists under your shingles but nowhere in your paperwork is, for premium purposes, at risk of not existing. The wider change is covered in the 2026 form guide.

What it is worth on the premium

The pattern is the same as every credited feature, and this site will not pretend to more precision than exists. Credits apply to the windstorm portion of the premium, which typically runs 30 to 60 percent of a Florida bill. The full stack of documented mitigation features commonly reduces that portion by 25 to 45 percent, with each feature's contribution set by the carrier's filed tables, which the April 2026 revision rebalanced to current building science. The obligation to credit documented features comes from s. 627.0629, F.S. and s. 627.711, F.S., and carriers have been required to describe their mitigation discounts on their websites since October 2023, so you can check how your own carrier credits SWR before a reroof decision.

It is also worth saying what the credit does not capture: the membrane's main value proposition is the loss it prevents. The premium credit is the recurring, visible part; the dry living room after a covering failure is the part the feature was actually designed for.

Grant money: SWR is on the list

Secondary water resistance is one of the four categories on the My Safe Florida Home program's eligible improvements list, alongside opening protection, roof-to-wall connection upgrades, and roof deck attachment upgrades. For qualifying low- and moderate-income homeowners, the program provides a free program-assigned wind mitigation inspection, then matches spending on improvements recommended in that report at 2:1 up to $10,000, with low-income homeowners eligible for up to $10,000 without the match. Reimbursement comes after completed, inspected work.

The program's sequencing rules matter here more than usual, because SWR rides on reroofs and reroofs do not wait politely. Only improvements recommended in the program inspection report are eligible, and work begun or contracts signed before grant approval are not matched. If you hope to pair grant money with a reroof, get the program inspection and grant approval lined up before the roofing contract is signed. Use any Florida state-certified contractor (no set program list since mid-2024) and verify the license at myfloridalicense.com. The full sequence is in the application walkthrough.

The reroof checklist, written down

Because SWR is decided inside someone else's construction schedule, the homeowner's job is mostly to be organized at the right week. Before the contract is signed: ask the contractor directly whether a self-adhering peel-and-stick underlayment is included, get the product name into the contract language, and, if you are pursuing My Safe Florida Home money, hold the signature until grant approval. During the job: get photographs of the membrane down on the deck before the covering goes over it, from enough angles that the coverage is evident. After the job: collect the permit closeout, the product approval documentation, and the final contract, and keep them with your house records. Then schedule the wind mitigation inspection, so the report on the current form captures the membrane alongside the deck nailing and anything else the project improved. None of these steps is difficult, and every one of them exists because the membrane will spend the rest of its life invisible. The week it goes down is the only week evidence is cheap.

The summary

Secondary water resistance is the quiet credit: a self-adhering membrane sealing your roof deck's seams, installed in the brief window when the deck is bare, and valuable precisely when everything above it has failed. It earns a credit on the wind mitigation form, it qualifies for My Safe Florida Home matching funds, and under the 2026 form it lives or dies by the photos and permits from the reroof that installed it. If a reroof is in your future, decide about peel-and-stick before the contract is signed, and document it while it can still be seen.
