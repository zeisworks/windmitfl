My Safe Florida Home (MSFH) is the state program that pays for wind mitigation twice over: first with a free program-assigned inspection, then with a matching grant for the improvements that inspection recommends. It is administered by the Florida Department of Financial Services, and everything official happens through one portal, mysafeflhome.com, which runs on the Neighborly Software platform. This guide walks the application from start to finish under the current cycle's rules, including the parts that trip people up.

First, confirm the program's status

MSFH is not an entitlement; it is open subject to available funding. The portal reopened on August 4, 2025 for the 2025-26 cycle, and demand exceeds funding. Applications queue by priority group, first-come first-served within each group, and once funding runs out, applicants are waitlisted for the next cycle. Before doing anything else, check the portal for the current status, because the program's open-or-paused state changes with appropriations.

Step one: eligibility, honestly assessed

The current cycle has hard rules, and several changed from earlier years. The home must be a site-built, single-family, owner-occupied primary residence with a homestead exemption. It must have been built before 2008 (the program rule references construction predating January 1, 2008). You must carry a valid homeowners insurance policy, and your insurance Coverage A, the dwelling coverage on your declarations page, must be at or below $700,000. That cap applies to every applicant this cycle, including low-income homeowners; the statute's low-income exemption from the cap is suspended through July 1, 2026.

The biggest change from the program's earlier years is income. The 2025-26 cycle is limited to low- and moderate-income homeowners. Low income means household income at or below 80 percent of your county's median income; moderate means above 80 and at or below 120 percent. Higher-income homeowners are not eligible this cycle. The dollar thresholds come from HUD's published income limits for each county, and the county pages on this site show the verified figures. A mobile home, a second home, a rental, an LLC-owned property, or a home built in 2008 or later will not qualify, and applying anyway just spends your time.

This site has a six-question eligibility checker that runs these rules locally in your browser.

Step two: understand your priority group

Since July 5, 2025, applications are ordered by priority group: first, low-income homeowners age 60 and older; second, low-income homeowners under 60; third, moderate-income homeowners age 60 and older; fourth, moderate-income homeowners under 60. For the first 60 days of a new application period, the priority groups apply; after that window, the general eligible pool opens. Within each group, it is first-come first-served. The practical implication: when a cycle opens, apply early. The queue position you establish matters.

Step three: the application itself

The application is filed on mysafeflhome.com. Expect to substantiate what the rules require: ownership and homestead status, the build year, your insurance policy, and household income for the income tier. Gather the documents before you start: your declarations page, your most recent income documentation for everyone in the household, and your property record. Incomplete applications do not hold a place in line the way completed ones do, so finishing in one sitting is the goal.

Step four: the free wind mitigation inspection

Approved applicants are assigned a wind mitigation inspection at no cost. This inspection is the gate for everything that follows: a grant can only fund improvements recommended in that inspection report, and no grant is issued before the inspection is completed. The inspector documents your home's existing features, the same categories the insurance form credits: roof shape, roof-to-wall attachment, roof deck attachment, secondary water resistance, building code era, and opening protection. You receive the report, and it doubles as a wind mitigation report you can hand to your insurer for any discounts your home already qualifies for, before any construction happens. One timing rule worth knowing: under the current statute, the inspection backing a grant application must have occurred within the previous 24 months, so an inspection report is not something to sit on indefinitely.

Step five: the grant, and the order of operations that matters

The grant is a 2:1 match: the state pays $2 for every $1 the homeowner spends, up to a maximum of $10,000. Low-income homeowners may receive up to $10,000 without the matching requirement. The money arrives as reimbursement after the work is completed and inspected. It is not a loan, and it is not an upfront payment.

Eligible improvements are the ones the program funds and your report recommends: opening protection (impact-rated windows, impact-rated exterior doors, hurricane shutters, and qualifying garage doors, for openings flagged in the inspection report), roof-to-wall connection upgrades, roof deck attachment upgrades, and secondary water resistance.

Now the rule that costs people real money when they miss it: do not sign a contract or begin work before grant approval. Work started early is work the program will not match. Since mid-2024 there is no set MSFH contractor list; you may use any Florida state-certified contractor. Verify any license at the Department of Business and Professional Regulation's portal, myfloridalicense.com, before signing anything, and be wary of anyone who pressures you to start before your approval is in hand.

Step six: after the work

Completed work is inspected, and then reimbursement is processed. After that, the improvements exist on paper in your program records, but your insurance discounts are not automatic. Get a wind mitigation report that reflects the finished work (under the form revised in April 2026, the inspector will want the permits and product approval numbers from your project, so keep every document), and submit it to your carrier. Among grant recipients who voluntarily reported outcomes, about 49 percent reported insurance savings, averaging roughly $981 per year. Treat those numbers as what they are, voluntary self-reports: savings are not guaranteed, and they typically follow comprehensive hardening rather than a single feature.

If you are waitlisted or the cycle closes

Because the program is funded in cycles and demand exceeds funding, two outcomes short of a grant are common and neither is a dead end. If you apply after funding for your group runs out, you are waitlisted for the next cycle rather than rejected; your eligibility was never the problem, the appropriation was. And if you complete the free inspection but the grant money has not reached you yet, you are in the program's inspection-to-grant queue, which the Legislature addressed directly in the budget passed in May 2026.

In either holding pattern, three things remain worth doing. Keep your contact information and documents current on the portal so nothing stalls when your number comes up. Use the inspection report you already have: it documents your home's existing features, and you can submit it to your insurance carrier now for whatever discounts those features already earn. And resist the urge to start construction; the no-work-before-approval rule does not bend for impatience, and starting early forfeits the match permanently.

The realistic timeline

Between application, inspection, approval, construction, final inspection, and reimbursement, this is a process measured in months, and the program has carried a queue of homeowners between the inspection and grant stages. There is a separate guide on why that backlog exists and what the 2026-27 budget does about it. The summary version: apply early in a cycle, complete the application in one pass, do not start work early, and keep every document. Those four habits are most of what a homeowner controls.
