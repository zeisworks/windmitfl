If you own a home in Florida, one piece of paper has an outsized effect on your insurance bill: the Uniform Mitigation Verification Inspection Form, known by its filing number, OIR-B1-1802. It is the document an inspector fills out to record the wind-resistant features of your house, and it is the document your insurance carrier uses to calculate the mitigation discounts that Florida law requires. On April 1, 2026, the state put a revised version of that form into effect (Rev. 04/26). It is the first major revision in over a decade, and it changes how credits are earned in ways every homeowner should understand before their next inspection or renewal.

Why the form exists at all

The form is not a courtesy from the insurance industry. It is the enforcement mechanism for two Florida statutes. Section 627.0629, Florida Statutes, requires residential property insurers to discount the windstorm portion of premiums based on documented mitigation features, and it directs the Office of Insurance Regulation to review and update the credited fixtures, techniques, and related discounts by January 1, 2025 and every five years after that. Section 627.711 requires insurers to offer those discounts when hurricane-resistant construction features are documented. Since October 1, 2023, the same law has also required every residential property insurer to describe its available hurricane mitigation discounts on its website.

So the chain works like this: the statute mandates discounts, the form is the proof, and the inspection is how the proof gets created. No form, no credits, no matter how strong the house actually is.

Where the 2026 revision came from

The 2026 revision grew out of the 2024 Residential Wind-Loss Mitigation Study commissioned by the Florida Office of Insurance Regulation, the periodic review the statute requires. The result is the first major overhaul of the form in over a decade, effective April 1, 2026. Carriers are expected to begin applying credits based on the new form around July 2026, so the period in between is a transition: new inspections happen on the new form while carriers finish updating their filed credit tables. The OIR maintains its own consumer materials on the form and the discounts at its wind mitigation resources page.

The big change: documentation, not box-checking

The headline difference in the 2026 form is evidentiary. Under the revised form, features that earn credit must be documented with photos, product approval numbers, and permit records. A feature that cannot be supported through a permit or a clear visual inspection generally will not receive credit, even if the homeowner is confident the feature is there.

That is a real shift in practice. Under the old regime, some credits were granted on findings that were difficult to audit. Under the new one, the burden is on the paper trail. For homeowners this cuts both ways. If your impact windows were installed with permits and the product approval numbers are recoverable, the new form documents them in a way carriers cannot easily dispute. If your upgrades were done without permits, or the records are gone, the inspector may be unable to credit them at all.

The discount tables themselves were also updated to current building science. Under the stricter criteria some features may earn more than they did before, and others less. That means a re-inspection under the new form is not guaranteed to beat your old report; it depends on which features your home has and how well they can be documented.

What stays the same

The basic mechanics carry over. A wind mitigation inspection is still performed by a licensed professional, still typically costs around $100 to $150, and the resulting report is still valid for up to five years, provided no material changes are made to the structure and no inaccuracies are found in the report. The credited feature categories are familiar ones: roof shape, roof-to-wall attachment, roof deck attachment, secondary water resistance, the building code era of the structure, and opening protection. Homes built under the 2001 Florida Building Code, which took effect March 1, 2002, or later earn a baseline credit from the code era alone.

And the most important constraint has not changed: discounts apply only to the windstorm portion of your premium. In Florida that portion typically runs 30 to 60 percent of the total bill, and documented features commonly reduce it by 25 to 45 percent. The exact numbers depend on your home, your county, and your carrier's filed credits, which is why no honest source will promise you a dollar figure before the form is in hand.

What happens to reports done before April 2026

Reports completed before April 2026 may remain valid for up to five years from their inspection date. You are not required to commission a new inspection just because the form changed. But there is a practical wrinkle: if you have made improvements since your last inspection, such as a new roof or new opening protection, those improvements are invisible to your old report. A new inspection under the updated form may capture credits the old report misses. There is a separate guide on exactly this decision: what to do when your report has expired or predates the new form.

What this means before your next inspection

The single most useful thing a homeowner can do under the 2026 form is gather records before the inspector arrives. Permits for the roof, windows, doors, and shutters. Product approval numbers for installed protection products. Receipts and contracts that identify what was installed and when. Under a form that credits what can be proven, the homeowner who shows up with a folder does better than the homeowner who shows up with a memory.

It is also worth being deliberate about timing. An inspection is a snapshot. If a reroof or an opening-protection project is already planned, inspecting after the work is finished and permitted lets one report capture everything, rather than paying for a second visit later.

Who fills the form out, and who pays

The form must be completed by a licensed professional: home inspectors, building contractors, architects, and engineers all perform wind mitigation inspections. The typical cost runs about $100 to $150, though prices may rise under the more documentation-heavy 2026 form, and the visit takes under an hour for most homes, longer under the new requirements. Whoever you hire, verify the license with the Department of Business and Professional Regulation at myfloridalicense.com before booking; the form is only as good as the license behind it.

For low- and moderate-income homeowners there is a way to get the inspection free. The My Safe Florida Home program, administered by the Department of Financial Services, assigns and pays for a wind mitigation inspection for eligible applicants, and then matches money spent on the improvements that inspection recommends, up to $10,000 on a 2:1 match. The program report serves the same documentation purpose with your carrier as one you paid for yourself. Eligibility for the current cycle is limited by income, property type, build year, and insured value; the application walkthrough covers the rules, and the program's official portal is mysafeflhome.com.

The honest summary

The 2026 form makes Florida's mitigation credit system stricter and more auditable. Documentation requirements went up. Credit tables were rebalanced. Carriers begin applying the new credits around July 2026. None of this changes the underlying deal written into the statutes: if your home has wind-resistant features and you can document them, the law requires your insurer to discount the windstorm portion of your premium. The form is how you collect on that deal, and the new version rewards homeowners who treat it like the legal document it is.
