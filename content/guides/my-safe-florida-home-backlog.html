Ask homeowners about My Safe Florida Home and you hear two stories. In one, the program paid for an inspection, matched thousands of dollars of hardening work, and cut the insurance bill. In the other, the homeowner applied, got the free inspection, and then waited. And waited. Both stories are true, and the gap between them is the program's backlog: the queue of homeowners who have completed inspections in hand but are still waiting for grant funding. This guide explains why that queue exists, who is in it, and what the budget Florida lawmakers passed in May 2026 is meant to do about it.

The backlog is structural, not a glitch

My Safe Florida Home is built as a two-stage program. Stage one is a free, program-assigned wind mitigation inspection. Stage two is a matching grant, up to $10,000 on a 2:1 state match, for improvements that inspection recommends. By rule, the inspection must come first: no grant is issued without a completed program inspection, and only improvements recommended in the report are eligible.

That design is sensible. It ensures the state pays only for work a documented inspection says the specific house needs. But it has an arithmetic consequence. Inspections are cheap and fast to deliver; grants are expensive. Each appropriation can therefore fund far more inspections than grants, and every funding cycle converts that difference into a queue of inspected, eligible homeowners waiting for grant money. When demand exceeds funding, which has been the program's normal condition, the queue grows until the next appropriation.

The program's own status language says this plainly: the program is open subject to available funding, demand exceeds funding, and once funding runs out, applicants are waitlisted for the next cycle.

Who waits, and in what order

Since July 5, 2025, the line is ordered by priority group: low-income homeowners age 60 and older first, then low-income homeowners under 60, then moderate-income homeowners 60 and older, then moderate-income homeowners under 60. Within each group it is first-come first-served. For the first 60 days of a new application period only the priority groups apply; after that, the general eligible pool opens.

The other major change arrived when the portal reopened on August 4, 2025 for the 2025-26 cycle: eligibility was limited to low- and moderate-income homeowners, defined as household income at or below 80 percent of county median income (low) and up to 120 percent (moderate). Higher-income homeowners, who were eligible in the program's earlier years, are not eligible this cycle. That change concentrated the program on the homeowners least able to fund hardening themselves, and it also reshaped the queue: the people waiting now are, by definition, low- and moderate-income households.

How big is the queue?

The most commonly cited count is roughly 45,000 homeowners with completed inspections awaiting grants. That figure comes from the Governor's February 2025 budget request, and it was still the number circulating in May 2026 coverage when lawmakers passed the reappropriation aimed at it. Treat it as a dated estimate rather than a live count: the Department of Financial Services has not published a fresher figure, and the 2025-26 cycle was expected to fund tens of thousands of grants, so the queue today is plausibly smaller. That is the deal this site makes everywhere: a number you can check, with its date attached, or no number.

What the 2026-27 budget does

On May 29, 2026, Florida lawmakers passed the state budget for fiscal year 2026-27 (HB 5001E), which reappropriates unused prior-year funds of roughly $378 million for My Safe Florida Home and about $27 million for the My Safe Florida Condominium Pilot, around $405 million combined, aimed largely at the inspection-to-grant backlog. As of this page's last verified date, that budget was awaiting the Governor's signature, which must come before the fiscal year begins July 1, and line-item vetoes remained possible.

Two details of that deal matter for anyone in the queue. First, the money is reappropriation, not new funding: unused funds from prior cycles being rolled forward rather than left on the table. Second, its stated target is the backlog itself, the homeowners who already hold completed inspections, rather than only new applicants. If the budget is signed as expected, the practical effect should be movement for people who have been parked between stage one and stage two.

Lawmakers also passed a separate tax package the same day (HB 7031E) with a three-year sales tax break on impact-resistant windows, doors, and garage doors. Two specifics worth knowing: it is structured as a refund you claim after purchase, not a discount at the register, and it covers purchases made between July 1, 2026 and June 30, 2029. Like the budget, it was awaiting the Governor's signature as of this page's last verified date.

If you are already in the queue

Your position is set by your priority group and your application date, and neither improves by reapplying. What you can do is make sure you are reachable and ready: keep your contact information current on mysafeflhome.com, keep your inspection report and income documents organized, and line up your decision-making in advance. Get contractor quotes you do not sign. Since mid-2024 the program has no set contractor list; any Florida state-certified contractor qualifies, and licenses can be verified at myfloridalicense.com. The one mistake that converts waiting into real loss is starting work early: improvements begun before grant approval are not matched. The queue tests patience; do not let it bait you into forfeiting the match.

One more thing worth doing while you wait: your program inspection report is a wind mitigation report. If it documents features your home already has, you can submit it to your insurance carrier now and pursue whatever discounts those existing features earn, independent of the grant. The waiting period for grant money does not have to be a waiting period for everything.

Why the program keeps outrunning its funding

It is worth being clear-eyed about why this keeps happening, because it is not mismanagement of a fixed pot; it is the shape of the problem. Florida's insurance statutes require carriers to discount the windstorm portion of premiums for documented mitigation, which means every hardened home has a recurring financial reward waiting for it. Among grant recipients who voluntarily reported outcomes, about 49 percent reported insurance savings, averaging roughly $981 a year, and while voluntary reporting overstates nothing so much as enthusiasm, numbers like that travel by word of mouth. A program offering a free inspection plus up to $10,000 in matching money, in a state where premiums are a kitchen-table issue, will reliably attract more eligible applicants than any single appropriation covers.

The income limits added for the 2025-26 cycle were one response, narrowing the pool to the households least able to self-fund. The reappropriation passed in May 2026 is another, converting unspent prior-year funds into grants for people already inspected. Whether the two together shrink the queue or merely steady it will show up in the program's status updates, which this site tracks on its county pages with dated changelog entries.

If you have not applied yet

The backlog is not a reason to skip applying; it is a reason to apply early and completely the moment a cycle is open. Funding arrives in waves tied to the state budget cycle, the queue is ordered first-come first-served within each priority group, and waitlisted applicants roll toward the next cycle. A homeowner who applies on day one of an open period, with documents in order, is in a categorically different position from one who applies in month four.
