Before wind can take a roof apart, it has to win an argument with nails. The roof deck, the sheathing panels that span your trusses and carry the shingles or tile above, is held down by its fasteners, and the wind mitigation form treats how that deck is attached as one of its credited feature categories. The specifics sound almost comically small for the money involved: what kind of nails, how long, and how far apart. But that is genuinely the credit, ring-shank nails and nailing pattern, and this guide explains what those terms mean, how the feature is inspected, and when the credit typically gets created.

What the deck is and why its fasteners matter

Strip away the shingles and underlayment and a roof is panels of structural sheathing nailed across the trusses. In high wind, the loads on those panels turn upward: the deck is being pulled off its framing. Whether it stays is decided by the withdrawal resistance of its fasteners, which comes down to three things: the type of nail, the size of the nail, and the spacing of the nailing pattern, meaning how many nails hold each panel along the framing it crosses.

A ring-shank nail has ridged rings along its shank rather than a smooth shaft. The rings bite into the wood and resist being pulled back out far better than a smooth-shank nail of the same size. Tighter spacing multiplies the effect: more nails per panel means more combined withdrawal resistance. That is the entire physics of this credit, and it is why the wind mitigation form's deck attachment category rates the combination of nail type and nailing pattern that an inspector can verify.

How the inspector verifies nails that are buried in a roof

From the attic. The deck's fasteners penetrate through the sheathing, and from below an inspector can observe the nails coming through, identify the type, measure penetration, and gauge the spacing along the framing. Expect photographs; under the form revision effective April 1, 2026, photo documentation is part of how every credited feature is supported, and a feature that cannot be documented through a permit or a clear visual inspection generally will not receive credit.

That documentation posture matters for deck attachment in a specific way. The nailing was done either when the house was built or when the roof was last replaced, and the cleanest evidence besides the attic view is the reroof permit record. If your roof was replaced, the permit and contract from that job tell the next inspector what was installed and when, and under the 2026 form that paper trail is the difference between a credited feature and an unprovable claim. The broader change is covered in the guide to the 2026 form.

One credit arrives by birthday: a home or roof built under the 2001 Florida Building Code, effective March 1, 2002, or later earns a baseline code-era credit, the modern code's fastening requirements being part of the reason. For older homes, the deck attachment category records whatever the attic evidence supports.

When the credit gets created: the reroof

Here is the practical heart of this guide. Almost nobody renails a roof deck as a standalone project, because the deck is only accessible when the roof covering is off. The deck attachment credit is therefore usually created during a reroof, when the old covering is stripped, the sheathing is exposed, and renailing to current standards is a routine part of the job. If your home will be reroofed in the next few years, that project is your one realistic shot at this category, alongside secondary water resistance and any roof-to-wall connection work, which share the same window of access. A reroof that handles the deck, the underlayment, and the connections converts one construction project into several documented credits at once.

Sequence the paperwork deliberately: permits pulled, product records kept, and the wind mitigation inspection scheduled after the project closes out, so a single report on the current form captures everything the project improved. A report is valid up to five years if nothing material changes, so the post-reroof report is the one you want carrying your renewals.

Money: the discount and the grant

Like every category on the form, deck attachment credits are applied by your carrier, from its filed tables, to the windstorm portion of your premium only. The windstorm portion typically runs 30 to 60 percent of a Florida bill, and the full stack of documented features commonly reduces that portion by 25 to 45 percent, with the exact contribution of deck attachment varying by carrier and by what the rest of the report shows. The 2026 revision rebalanced the credit tables to current building science. The legal backbone is the same pair of statutes behind every credit: s. 627.0629, F.S. mandates windstorm discounts for documented mitigation, and s. 627.711, F.S. requires insurers to offer them.

For low- and moderate-income homeowners, roof deck attachment upgrades are on the My Safe Florida Home eligible improvements list. The program provides a free program-assigned inspection, and then matches spending on recommended improvements 2:1 up to $10,000, with low-income homeowners eligible for up to $10,000 without the matching requirement. The rules are strict about order: inspection first, grant approval before any contract is signed or work begins, reimbursement after completed and inspected work. Use any Florida state-certified contractor (there has been no set program contractor list since mid-2024) and verify the license at myfloridalicense.com. The full process is in the application walkthrough.

Two questions homeowners ask

My roof was just replaced. Do I get the credit automatically? No. Nothing about your premium changes until a wind mitigation report documents the new work and your carrier applies its filed credits. The reroof creates the feature; the inspection creates the credit. Schedule the inspection after the project closes out, hand over the permit and product records, and then submit the completed form to your carrier. A home or roof built under the 2001 Florida Building Code, effective March 1, 2002, or later also carries the baseline code-era credit, and a post-reroof report is how that gets onto paper too.

My old report already shows good deck attachment. Do I need to do anything? Check two dates. If the report is within its five-year validity and the house is unchanged, it keeps working; you are not required to re-inspect because the form changed in April 2026. If the report has lapsed, or the roof has been replaced since it was written, the documented credit no longer matches the house, and a new inspection on the current form is what restores it. The decision cases are laid out in the guide to expired and pre-2026 reports.

The summary

Roof deck attachment is the least glamorous credit on the form and one of the most mechanical: ring-shank nails grip wood better than smooth ones, more nails grip better than fewer, and an inspector in your attic can see exactly which argument your roof will make. The credit usually gets created during a reroof, documented through the permit trail, and applied by your carrier against the windstorm portion of your premium. If a reroof is coming, plan for this category on purpose. If one just happened, make sure a current wind mitigation report actually captured it.
