Opening protection is the wind mitigation credit homeowners most often think they have and do not. The reason is a single rule with no partial credit: on Florida's wind mitigation form, opening protection is scored all-or-nothing. Every opening in the building envelope must carry qualifying protection for the home to earn the credit. Impact windows on the front of the house and ordinary glass on the back is, for credit purposes, the same as no protection at all. This guide explains what qualifies, what does not, how the rule plays out in practice, and how both the insurance credit and the grant money work.

Why openings get their own category

Windows, doors, garage doors, and skylights are the soft points of the building envelope. When an opening fails in a storm, wind enters the building, and the structure must then resist pressure from inside as well as outside, which is how localized breakage escalates into structural damage. Protecting openings keeps the envelope closed, which is why opening protection sits alongside the roof categories as one of the form's credited features, and why the form is unforgiving about coverage: an envelope with one unprotected opening is not closed.

What qualifies, and what does not

Two families of protection earn the credit: impact-rated glazing (impact glass) and rated exterior shutters. Both are products with documented ratings, which under the 2026 form matters more than ever. Interior treatments do not qualify; protection is judged at the envelope, from the outside.

The all-or-nothing rule then does the sorting. Every opening, meaning every window, every exterior door, the garage door, and the skylights, must carry qualifying protection for the credit to apply. The practical failure cases are familiar to every inspector: the house with impact windows everywhere except one bathroom, the protected home with an unrated garage door, the shutter set missing one panel. Each of those homes paid for most of an opening protection credit and collects none of it.

One geographic note. In Miami-Dade and Broward counties, the Florida Building Code's High-Velocity Hurricane Zone provisions apply, and product approvals for opening protection are stricter than in the rest of the state. If you are buying products in those counties, the product approval documentation is its own subject, and it is exactly what the inspector will ask for.

How the credit is documented after April 2026

The form revised effective April 1, 2026 (OIR-B1-1802, Rev. 04/26) raised the documentation standard for every credited feature, and opening protection is where homeowners will feel it most. Credited features must be supported by photos, product approval numbers, and permit records, and features that cannot be documented through a permit or a clear visual inspection generally will not receive credit.

Impact products carry their ratings in their paperwork and labeling, so the evidence is collectable if you collect it. For each protected opening, that means: the product approval number, the permit from the installation, and the label or documentation tying the product to your house. A house full of genuinely impact-rated glass with no recoverable documentation is in a weak position under the new form. If your products were installed with permits, those records exist; gather them before the inspection rather than during it. The broader revision is covered in the 2026 form guide.

What the credit is worth

Opening protection is one of the form's major credits, and the same honest arithmetic applies to it as to every other. Credits are applied by your carrier, from its filed tables, to the windstorm portion of your premium, which typically runs 30 to 60 percent of a Florida bill. The full stack of documented mitigation features commonly reduces that portion by 25 to 45 percent. The April 2026 revision updated the credit tables to current building science, so values shifted and vary by carrier. The legal requirement that documented features be credited comes from s. 627.0629, F.S. and s. 627.711, F.S., and carriers must describe their available discounts on their websites, a requirement in effect since October 2023.

The all-or-nothing rule reshapes the economics of partial projects. Protecting half the openings buys real physical protection for those openings, but the premium credit arrives only at completion. If you are phasing a project for budget reasons, plan the phases to reach full coverage, and do not expect the discount until the last opening is done. The final opening you protect is, in credit terms, the most valuable one.

Grant money: the program funds exactly this

Opening protection is first on the My Safe Florida Home program's eligible improvements list: impact-rated windows, impact-rated exterior doors, hurricane shutters, and qualifying garage doors, for openings flagged in the program's inspection report. For qualifying low- and moderate-income homeowners, the program provides a free wind mitigation inspection and matches improvement spending 2:1 up to $10,000, with low-income homeowners eligible for up to $10,000 without the match. Reimbursement follows completed, inspected work.

The sequencing rules are strict and worth repeating, because opening protection is the program's most common project type. The program inspection comes first. Only openings flagged in that report are eligible. Grant approval comes before any contract is signed or work begins; work started early is not matched. Since mid-2024 there is no set program contractor list, so any Florida state-certified contractor may do the work; verify licenses at myfloridalicense.com, and be skeptical of any salesperson who urges you to sign now and sort the grant later. The full process is in the application walkthrough, and lawmakers have also moved on a sales tax exemption for impact windows and doors, though its enactment and effective dates were unconfirmed as of this page's last verified date.

Audit your own house: the opening inventory

Before paying for an inspection or pricing a project, walk the outside of your house with a notepad and count every opening: each window, each exterior door, the garage door, and any skylights. For each one, note what protects it and what documentation you hold. Impact glazing should have label or paperwork evidence and, for installed products, a permit. Shutters count only if they are rated exterior products; a stack of plywood in the garage is not opening protection on the form, and neither is any interior treatment. The exercise takes twenty minutes and answers the only question the credit asks: is coverage total?

The inventory usually lands in one of three places. Everything is protected and documented: book the inspection, because the credit is sitting there waiting for paperwork. Everything is protected except one or two openings: you now have a precise, small project that unlocks the whole credit, often the highest-leverage spend available on the house. Coverage is partial or undocumented: you have a phasing plan to build and a records hunt to start with your local permit office, in either order. In all three cases the inventory costs nothing and replaces guesswork with a list.

The summary

Opening protection earns one of the wind mitigation form's major credits under one unforgiving condition: total coverage. Impact glass or rated exterior shutters on every opening, garage door and skylights included, documented with product approvals and permits that the 2026 form now demands. Audit your own house honestly before assuming you qualify, finish the last opening before expecting the discount, and if you are eligible for My Safe Florida Home, let the program inspection define the project before any contract is signed.
