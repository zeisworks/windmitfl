When wind pushes on a house, the roof is not just pressed sideways; it is lifted. The connection between the roof structure and the walls is what resists that uplift, and Florida's wind mitigation form treats it as one of its credited feature categories for exactly that reason. The credited hardware comes in two familiar names, hurricane clips and straps, and the difference between them, and between either and plain nailed connections, is one of the things a wind mitigation inspector climbs into your attic to determine. This guide explains what the inspector is looking at, how the credit works, and how this is one of the features you can actually buy.

What the connection is

Your roof's trusses or rafters rest on the top of the exterior walls. Something has to hold them there against uplift. In the weakest version, that something is toe-nails: nails driven at an angle through the truss into the wall plate. Metal connectors improve on this. A hurricane clip is a piece of metal hardware that ties the truss to the wall plate. A strap is a longer metal connector that wraps over or around the truss and fastens down the wall framing, engaging more of the structure in resisting uplift. On the wind mitigation form, the credited category is roof-to-wall attachment, and clips or straps are the hardware that earns it, with the classification depending on what the inspector can actually observe and document on your connections.

How it gets inspected and documented

This feature lives in your attic, which is why a wind mitigation inspection involves a flashlight and insulation dust. The inspector examines the connections where trusses meet walls, identifies the hardware, and photographs it. Under the form revision effective April 1, 2026, the documentation standard tightened across every category: credited features must be supported by photos, product approval numbers, and permit records where applicable, and a feature that cannot be documented through a permit or clear visual inspection generally will not receive credit.

For roof-to-wall attachment that has a specific practical meaning: if your connections are buried under insulation, hidden by construction, or otherwise not visible, the inspector may be unable to credit hardware that is genuinely there. If you have had connection upgrades done, the permit records from that work are now first-class evidence; keep them and hand them to the inspector. The general shift is covered in the guide to what changed on the 2026 form.

What the credit is worth

As with every feature on the form, the credit is applied by your carrier from its filed tables, to the windstorm portion of your premium only. That windstorm portion typically runs 30 to 60 percent of a Florida premium, and the full set of documented mitigation features commonly reduces it by 25 to 45 percent; roof-to-wall attachment is one of the larger structural contributors inside that stack. The 2026 revision updated the credit tables to current building science, so the exact values shifted, and they differ by carrier. The legal floor does not shift: s. 627.0629, F.S. requires insurers to discount the windstorm portion for documented mitigation, and s. 627.711, F.S. requires them to offer the discounts when features are documented. Carriers have also been required since October 2023 to describe their mitigation discounts on their websites, so you can see how yours treats this category.

One credit in this neighborhood comes free with age, or rather youth: a home or roof built under the 2001 Florida Building Code, effective March 1, 2002, or later earns a baseline code-era credit, because the modern code's connection requirements are part of why newer homes rate better. If your home predates that era, the attachment category is where upgrades can move your rating toward what the code era would have given you.

Upgrading: the buyable structural credit

Unlike roof shape, roof-to-wall attachment can be retrofitted. Connection upgrades are typically performed from the attic side or during a reroof, when the framing is accessible. Two funding facts matter here.

First, roof-to-wall connection upgrades are on the My Safe Florida Home program's eligible improvements list. For low- and moderate-income homeowners who qualify, the program provides a free wind mitigation inspection and then matches improvement spending 2:1 up to $10,000, with low-income homeowners eligible for up to $10,000 without the match. The order of operations is strict: the program inspection comes first, only improvements recommended in that report are eligible, and work begun before grant approval is not matched. The full sequence is in the application walkthrough.

Second, whoever pays, use a licensed contractor and pull permits. Since mid-2024 the MSFH program has no set contractor list; any Florida state-certified contractor may do the work, and licenses are verifiable at myfloridalicense.com. The permit is not bureaucratic overhead. Under the 2026 form, the permit record is part of how the upgrade becomes a credit, and an unpermitted connection job may be invisible to the next inspection.

Find out what you have before paying anyone

Most homeowners genuinely do not know what is holding their roof to their walls, and finding out is cheaper than assuming. Start with paper: your existing wind mitigation report, if you have one, records the attachment classification on its roof-to-wall line, and the permit history from any past roof or structural work may name the hardware. If the paper trail is empty, the answer is in the attic, at the line where the trusses meet the exterior walls; metal connectors are visible there when they exist and accessible. A wind mitigation inspection settles the question authoritatively for around $100 to $150 and documents every other credited category in the same visit, which is the sensible order of operations: know the classification first, then decide whether an upgrade is worth pricing. Paying for connection work your attic turns out to already have, or skipping a credit your hardware already earns but your carrier has never seen documented, are both failures of the same cheap step.

The reroof window

If a reroof is anywhere in your plans, know that it is the single best moment to deal with this category. With the roof covering off, connections are accessible in ways they never are otherwise, and attachment work can ride on the same project and permit trail. A reroof that ignores the connections is a missed window that will not reopen for the life of the new roof. Coordinate the wind mitigation inspection for after the project closes out, so one report captures the new covering, the deck nailing, any underlayment, and the connection hardware all at once.

The summary

Roof-to-wall attachment is the form's answer to uplift: toe-nails at the bottom of the scale, clips better, straps better still, judged by what an inspector can see and document in your attic. It is one of the few structural credits a homeowner can buy outright, it is on the MSFH eligible improvements list, and under the 2026 form its value depends on a clean paper trail. If your report is old, your attic has hardware your paperwork does not show, or a reroof is coming, this category is worth your attention before your next renewal.
