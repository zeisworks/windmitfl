A wind mitigation inspection is the cheapest document in Florida homeownership relative to what it can do. The typical local cost runs about $100 to $150, the visit usually takes under an hour for most homes, and the resulting report is valid for up to five years provided nothing material changes on the house. Set against an insurance system where the windstorm portion is often 30 to 60 percent of the premium, and where documented features commonly cut that portion by 25 to 45 percent, the inspection is the rare purchase whose entire purpose is to lower a recurring bill. This guide covers what it costs, what actually happens during one, and how the form revision that took effect in April 2026 changed the visit.

What it costs and why prices may drift up

The going rate has long sat around $100 to $150 for a standard single-family home. Two honest caveats. First, prices are set by individual licensed inspectors in a competitive market, so quotes vary by area and by how complicated your home is to inspect. Second, the form revised effective April 1, 2026 (OIR-B1-1802, Rev. 04/26) requires substantially more documentation per inspection: photos, product approval numbers, and permit records for credited features. More documentation per visit means more inspector time per visit, and prices may rise to reflect that. When comparing quotes, the question worth asking is not just the price but whether the inspector will pull and attach the permit history, because under the new form the paperwork is where credits live or die.

Note what the fee does not include: the inspection does not fix anything, and it does not guarantee a discount. It documents what your home already has. If the home has no creditable features beyond its code era, the report will say so.

Who performs it

Wind mitigation inspections are performed by licensed professionals: home inspectors, building contractors, architects, and engineers. Whoever you hire, verify the license with the Department of Business and Professional Regulation at myfloridalicense.com before booking. If you are approved for the My Safe Florida Home program, the program assigns and pays for the inspection itself; that report serves the same purpose and costs you nothing.

What the inspector looks at

The inspection follows the categories on the form, because the form is the deliverable. Six features carry the credits:

  • Roof shape. The geometry of the roof, where a hip roof earns the strongest shape credit.
  • Roof-to-wall attachment. How the roof structure is fastened to the walls, with hurricane clips or straps as the credited hardware.
  • Roof deck attachment. How the decking is fastened to the trusses, where ring-shank nails and the nailing pattern determine the rating.
  • Secondary water resistance. Whether a peel-and-stick underlayment sits beneath the roof covering.
  • Building code era. Whether the roof or home was built under the 2001 Florida Building Code, effective March 1, 2002, or later, which earns a baseline credit on its own.
  • Opening protection. Impact glass or rated exterior shutters, judged under an all-or-nothing rule: every opening must be protected for the credit, and interior treatments do not qualify.

Physically, expect the inspector to spend time in the attic, because that is where the roof-to-wall connections, the deck nailing, and any underlayment evidence are visible. Expect photographs throughout: attic framing, the connections, the roof covering, the openings, and labels on windows, doors, and shutters. Under the 2026 form those photos are not optional color; they are the evidence the carrier's underwriter reviews.

What changed about the visit in 2026

The revised form raised the documentation bar. Features that earn credit must now be supported by photos, product approval numbers, and permit records, and features that cannot be documented through a permit or a clear visual inspection generally will not receive credit. The discount tables were also updated to current building science, so some features may earn more and others less under the stricter criteria.

For the homeowner, this changes your role in the inspection. Before the visit, gather what you have: permits for the roof and any window, door, or shutter work; product approval numbers; contracts and receipts that identify products and dates. An inspector can document a feature far more easily when the paper trail is handed over, and a credit that exists in your filing cabinet but not in the inspection report does not exist at all as far as your premium is concerned. The full revision is covered in the guide to what changed on the 2026 form.

After the report: how the money part works

You submit the completed form to your insurance carrier, which applies its filed credits to the windstorm portion of your premium. Two statutes stand behind this: s. 627.0629, F.S. mandates the discounts for documented features, and s. 627.711, F.S. requires insurers to offer them. Since October 2023, carriers must also describe their available mitigation discounts on their websites, which makes it possible to see your own carrier's credit structure before you spend anything.

Keep expectations calibrated. The discount applies to the windstorm portion only, not the whole premium, and the size depends on which features the report documents and your carrier's filed credit table. The stakes scale with where you live: among the counties this site covers, the Office of Insurance Regulation's January 2026 Stability Report puts the average homeowners premium including wind coverage anywhere from about $2,758 in Pasco to $6,412 in Palm Beach, and each county page shows the local figure. The savings estimator on this site does the arithmetic across the typical ranges so you can see what an inspection could plausibly be worth for your premium before you book one.

Booking one: what to ask before you pay

Four questions separate a useful booking call from a coin flip. First, what license does the inspector hold, so you can verify it at myfloridalicense.com before the visit. Second, is the inspection performed on the current form, OIR-B1-1802 Rev. 04/26; any answer other than yes is disqualifying for an inspection performed after April 1, 2026. Third, does the fee include pulling permit history and attaching product approval documentation, or is the homeowner expected to supply it; either answer can be fine, but you need to know which job is yours. Fourth, how is the completed form delivered, since you will be submitting it to your carrier and may want it again at your next renewal or sale.

And before anyone arrives, do the free part yourself: locate your permits, product approval paperwork, and contracts for the roof and any opening protection. Under the 2026 form, a feature that cannot be documented through a permit or clear visual inspection generally will not receive credit, so the folder you hand the inspector is not a courtesy. It is part of the inspection.

When the math favors waiting

Because a report is a five-year snapshot, timing matters. If a reroof is planned for next year, an inspection today documents the old roof; you would need another inspection after the work to capture the new credits. Homeowners mid-project usually do best inspecting once, after the work is completed and permitted. Conversely, if your home already has documented features and your current report is several years old or predates improvements, an inspection now starts the clock on five years of credited renewals.
